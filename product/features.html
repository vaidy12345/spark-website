Features

From “I have a pile of recordings” to “my students have a real course”.

Spark is a production line for serious teaching: it listens once, then quietly does the unglamorous work of structuring, summarizing, questioning, and tutoring—so your students can learn in short, focused sessions.

1. Course structure: chapters and micro‑sections from a single long video

Upload one messy recording and Spark breaks it into a course: high‑level chapters, each made of short 3–8 minute sections. Students don’t have to scrub through a two‑hour wall of video—they can jump directly to the idea they care about.

Behind the scenes, Spark pays attention to where ideas start and end in your speech, not just where there happen to be silences. It looks for topic shifts, repeated reminders, and “now let’s move on to…” signals that human teachers use naturally.

The result is a clean outline you can skim at a glance, plus a timeline with precise chapter boundaries so students always land in the right place.

2. Study notes: readable, math‑friendly summaries for each chapter

For every chapter, Spark drafts notes that feel like a careful teaching assistant was listening: section headings, key ideas, worked examples, and formulas that render cleanly instead of turning into broken symbols.

Each note is checked against what you actually said. If the system is unsure, it prefers to flag the note for review rather than confidently inventing details. When you see a small “verified” badge, it means that note has passed an extra layer of scrutiny.

Students get a place where they can skim before class, catch up after class, or quickly revise the night before an exam without re‑watching everything.

3. Quizzes: chapter‑level checks today, per‑student adaptation soon

Spark generates chapter‑level quizzes that are aligned with what you actually taught—complete with correct answers and explanations. You can run them live at the start of class, assign them between sessions, or let students self‑check on their own time.

Instead of random trivia, questions are chosen to cover the right spread of ideas. If something doesn’t clearly map back to your content, it simply doesn’t make it into the final quiz.

Coming soon: per‑student quizzes that adapt based on how each learner has been answering so far—leaning more on topics they struggle with and giving gentle wins where they are strong.

4. Course‑specific AI Tutor: a coach that only talks about your material

Every course in Spark can have its own tutor. Students can ask “why” and “how” questions, request another example, or get nudged towards the exact chapter and section that clears up their confusion.

The tutor doesn’t improvise from the wider internet. It answers from the course’s own notes and structure, with short references back to where in the material the answer is coming from.

Over time, this becomes a patient, always‑on extension of you—the same voice, the same notation, the same way of thinking, just available at midnight before a test.

5. “Search my teacher’s knowledge base”: search across everything you’ve taught

As more of your videos become courses, Spark quietly builds a private knowledge base on top: a way for students to search across subjects, chapters, and time without leaving your world.

A student can type “optics last month” or “integration by parts in Grade 12 physics” and get pointed to the right chapters, not to a random search engine result.

It’s less like searching the web, and more like asking a well‑organized version of you: “Where did you explain this best?”

6. Assignments (upcoming): from “I watched it” to “I can actually do it”

For coding‑heavy courses, Spark will be able to suggest real assignments—bug‑fixing, completion, refactoring tasks—based on the chapters a student has just covered. Solutions can be checked automatically with structured feedback on correctness and style.

Over time, the same idea extends beyond code: case studies, “what would you do?” scenarios, and open‑ended prompts that force learners to apply what they’ve picked up, not just repeat it.

The goal is simple: a steady drip of doing, not just watching and reading—tuned to where each student currently is.
