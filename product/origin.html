Origin story

How a decade of almost‑right products led to Spark.

Spark is not a weekend side‑project. It is the latest step in a long, messy journey: building for real teachers and students, finding the edges of each idea in the wild, and then keeping the learnings while we try again.

“Everybody has a plan until they get punched in the face.”

— Mike Tyson

Our plan started with “AI makes your marketing content for you.” What happened in real classes, studios, and coaching calls forced us to rethink where AI should actually live.

Before we ever touched education products, we spent nine years running a small software studio called Multunus—building and maintaining other people’s products. That decade taught us how expensive broken systems are, how much reliability matters when people depend on your software, and left us with one unresolved itch: to stop building only for others and pour that craft into our own product.

2017–2022 · From Good Karma to PracticeNow – trying to keep people showing up

The product journey started with a simple but emotionally loaded problem: helping people stay consistent with their wellness practice. That led to an early product called Good Karma, and then to building tools for yoga and wellness teachers—tracking subscriptions, attendance, payments, and hybrid class logistics across in‑person and online students.

Over time that work grew into a dedicated product called PracticeNow: an operations backbone for independent teachers, handling recurring classes, workshops, recordings, international payments, country‑wise pricing, and student communication from one place.

The lesson from this phase was clear: teachers will trust you with their payments, rosters, and schedules when you remove real friction. The relationship between teacher and student is the center of everything; our software had to quietly support that bond, not get in the way.

2022–2024 · Storyteller & AI Reels – when “perfect” AI plans met real classrooms

After PracticeNow, the next obvious question was: if we already run the operations, can we also help teachers grow faster? First came Storyteller—an attempt to nurture leads with automated WhatsApp sequences built from a teacher’s existing content. On paper, it made sense. In practice, we kept seeing the same pattern: unless the content was genuinely useful and easy to consume, no amount of automation could save it.

So we stepped closer to the content itself. The idea behind our AI reels product sounded almost too good: connect your Zoom account, and we quietly scan your long classes for the best moments—clear explanations, satisfying “aha” reactions, strong audio—and turn them into vertical clips with captions, covers, and ready‑to‑post copy. No editing timelines, no rewriting hooks, no exporting. Just teach the way you already do, and wake up to a stack of reels and shorts.

In demos, it was magic. Teachers saw their own words come back as polished clips and said things like, “You mean I never have to record reels again?” But the moment we tried to live with it in real classes, the cracks showed up fast.

Zoom recordings looked grainy and flat on social media; more than one teacher told us they would rather post nothing than something that made them look unprofessional. Asking teachers to record a second, phone‑based angle sounded simple; in hybrid studios it turned into a mess of extra tripods, two clip‑on mics, and constant anxiety about which device was actually getting good audio. Even when the hardware cooperated, the upload flow from high‑resolution phones into a browser‑based tool was painful enough that people quietly stopped doing it.

Worst of all, the product changed how some teachers felt while teaching. Knowing that “the AI” was always watching nudged them out of the relaxed, responsive headspace their students loved and into a vague sense of performing for a camera that might or might not behave. On our side, the graphs told the story brutally: excited demos, a few early reels shipped, then usage tapering off to almost nothing.

We did not walk away thinking “AI for creators doesn’t work.” We walked away thinking we were pointing the AI at the wrong problem. The real magic in those long sessions was not how they would look chopped into a few seconds of social media—it was how much teaching was packed into them, and how little of that teaching students could reliably reuse.

The algorithms we built to find strong moments in 60–90 minute recordings did not get thrown away. They became the backbone of how Spark now finds chapters and micro‑sections. The painful part was everything we had to admit in order to make that shift.

What it costs to keep changing your mind (and why we did it anyway)

Every pivot here came with a real cost: months of design and engineering thrown away, features we were proud of that never made it to a launch video, and more than one conversation about whether we should just “double down on what we already have.”

But every time we sat with real teachers and students, the same thing happened: the data from the classroom did not match the story in our slide decks. We could either protect our ego and keep pushing the old idea, or accept the hit, keep the learnings, and move one step closer to a product that actually helps people learn.

Spark is what you get when you choose the second option, over and over again.

2025– · Spark – turning long teaching into micro‑learning that sticks

The real breakthrough came when we stopped looking at sessions only as marketing fuel and started looking at them as dense learning material. Lectures, workshops, cohort sessions, long‑form YouTube videos, and internal trainings are all full of new ideas, definitions, frameworks, and worked examples. They are perfect raw material for micro‑learning and tutoring—if you can reshape them.

Two early academic teachers agreed to an unusual deal: no revenue at first, but open access to thousands of hours of their Zoom lectures. That library became the test bed for what eventually evolved into Spark’s current engine—turning raw recordings into structured courses with chapters, short sections, notes, slides, quizzes, and a course‑specific tutor that stays inside the teacher’s universe.

A single 90‑minute session can turn into a dozen or more short sections. Each can be completed in under ten minutes with notes and a quick check for understanding. Students make progress in small, repeatable steps; teachers get a durable library instead of a pile of forgotten recordings.

For teachers who already teach through long‑form video—on Zoom, YouTube, or even folders of recordings on Google Drive—Spark is the first version where everything points directly at learning: micro‑learning, frequent assessment, and a grounded tutor working together. It is the first time teachers told us, “This feels like how I always wanted my students to be able to use my content.”

What happens to PracticeNow?

For years, the operations layer behind Spark has been available publicly as PracticeNow: plans, subscriptions, payments, virtual studio, secure video, promotions, and reporting for live and recorded classes.

Over the coming months, those monetization and operations features will be folded directly into Spark as core capabilities. The goal is simple: teachers and students experience a single, seamless product—from “turn my recordings into micro‑courses” all the way to “run and grow my teaching business,” with an upcoming AI tutor that stays firmly grounded in the teacher’s own content.
