Reporting

Less guessing, more watching your students learn in real time.

Spark’s reporting is built to answer one question: “What should I do with my next hour of teaching time?” It shows you which students and which concepts need attention, instead of just showing you vanity graphs.

1. Live quiz reports: see the class thinking, not just the final score

When you run a quiz in class, Spark shows you a live report as answers come in:

  • A per‑student view: correct, wrong, skipped, total questions, and time taken.
  • A per‑question view: how many got it right, how many struggled, and which questions are slowing everyone down.
  • A question‑by‑question breakdown you can use immediately to decide what to revisit before moving on.

The goal is not to rank your students, but to give you a clear picture of where the group is stuck while there’s still time to fix it.

2. Per‑student progress over time (upcoming)

Today, Spark already captures rich quiz data: which student chose which option, how long each question took, and which chapters each question belongs to. The next step is to give you a simple, longitudinal view per student:

  • A timeline of quizzes taken, with scores and completion times.
  • A topic‑level view of strengths and weak spots across your subjects.
  • Plain‑language suggestions like “Spend more time on waves” or “Redo the optics quiz from last week”.

This isn’t about generating more charts—it’s about giving you and the student a shared, simple picture of how things are changing.

3. Turning data into concrete teaching decisions

All of Spark’s reporting is built to feed back into how you run your next class:

  • Start each session with a short quiz, then use the live report to decide whether to revise or move on.
  • Spot questions nearly everyone got wrong and re‑teach those ideas with new examples.
  • Encourage students to review their attempts and then ask the course tutor about questions they missed.

Over time, this creates a gentle loop: quiz → see where the class really is → adjust → quiz again. Students feel seen, and you waste less time guessing.
